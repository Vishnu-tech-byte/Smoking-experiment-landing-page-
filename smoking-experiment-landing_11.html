The Experiment

A food stall. Disturbing packaging. A mirror held up to human behavior.

Cigarette packs in India carry some of the most graphic health warnings in the world. Images of mouth cancer, blackened lungs, throat tumors. And yet, millions of packs are sold every day.

We want to understand why. So we are running a real experiment at an IT office campus in Chennai.

The Setup

A small food stall featuring a well-known brand is placed near a smoking zone. The food items are packaged with graphic warning images similar to cigarette pack warnings.

The Observation

We observe whether people choose to buy food despite disturbing images on the packaging, mirroring how cigarette buyers behave.

The Question

Do warning images actually change behavior? What does behavioral science tell us about why people ignore graphic warnings?

The Video

The footage and insights become a YouTube video exploring addiction, decision-making, and the limits of public health messaging.

What exactly happens during the experiment?

Our team sets up a small food stall near your office's smoking zone. The food items on display are packaged with graphic warning images similar to the ones found on cigarette packs. We observe and film how people respond to the stall: do they approach it, do they hesitate, do they still buy despite the warnings? We also conduct brief voluntary conversations with willing participants about their decision-making. The entire session runs for 3 to 4 hours, and all footage is reviewed before being used in the final video.

Do we need to provide anything at all?

No. We bring everything we need: the stall, the products, the camera equipment, and our team. All we need from you is written permission to use the agreed space and a point of contact to coordinate entry and timing. You do not need to arrange food, furniture, power, or anything else. We set up independently and clear out completely when done.

Will our employees be pressured to participate?

Absolutely not. This is a passive observation experiment. Employees can walk past, ignore the stall entirely, or choose to engage. Nobody will be approached, asked to respond to camera, or followed up with unless they voluntarily choose to interact. Anyone who does interact will be asked for consent before any footage of them is used. This is a no-pressure environment by design.

Will our company name appear in the video?

Only if you want it to. We are happy to either acknowledge your company by name in the video as a partner who made this experiment possible, or keep your involvement anonymous entirely. We discuss this with you during the onboarding call and confirm your preference in writing before filming begins. The choice is completely yours.

What if we agree now but want to back out later?

You can pull out at any stage before filming begins, with no hard feelings. If you fill in the form and we are in the coordination phase, just let us know and we will remove you from the plan. The only exception is once filming has already started on your premises. In that case, we can still discuss what footage can and cannot be used. We will always try to respect your decision and find a reasonable solution.

Raw footage that is not used in the final edit is not shared publicly. We do not license the footage to third parties without separate written permission from you.

No. We operate entirely within the smoking zone area or a nearby outdoor space that you designate. We do not enter office floors, meeting rooms, or any internal work areas.
